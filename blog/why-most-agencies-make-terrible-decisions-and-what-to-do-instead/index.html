Why Most Agencies Make Terrible Decisions (and What to Do Instead)

Agencies Are Weirdly Bad at Decisions

Agencies sell decision-making as a service. Clients pay them to figure out what to run, where to spend, and how to adjust. And yet, most agencies make consistently terrible decisions about their own businesses. They chase bad-fit clients, hold onto underperformers too long, and copy whatever the loudest person on Twitter said last week.

This isn't because agency owners are stupid. It's because the incentive structure of agency life creates blind spots that are hard to see from the inside.

The Revenue Trap

The most destructive pattern in agencies is keeping bad clients because you need the revenue. Everyone does it. A client is a nightmare to work with, the results aren't great, and your team dreads every meeting. But they pay $5K/month and you need that to make payroll.

So you keep them. And keeping them creates a cascade of bad decisions. Your team burns out on work that doesn't produce results. Your case studies suffer because your best work is being diluted by mediocre accounts. Your reputation takes hits from clients who were never a good fit in the first place.

The counterintuitive truth is that firing your worst client almost always leads to better revenue within 90 days. The capacity you free up gets filled by better work, your team performs better, and your positioning sharpens because you're not trying to be everything to everyone.

Decision Frameworks That Actually Work

Good agency decisions come from having clear criteria established before the situation demands a choice. When you're in the middle of a crisis, you make emotional decisions. When you've pre-decided your criteria, you make rational ones.

  • Client acceptance criteria — what has to be true before you say yes? Write it down.
  • Firing triggers — what specific conditions mean a client needs to go? Define them now, not in the moment.
  • Hiring benchmarks — at what revenue level do you add what role? Don't hire reactively.
  • Service scope boundaries — what do you do and what do you refuse? Scope creep kills margins.

The Comparison Problem

Agency owners spend too much time looking at what other agencies are doing and not enough time looking at their own data. Someone on a podcast says "we switched to performance-based pricing and tripled revenue." So you think about switching your pricing model. But you don't know their margins, their churn rate, their client mix, or whether they're even telling the truth.

Your business has its own data. Your close rate, your churn, your average client lifetime value, your team's capacity. Those numbers should drive your decisions, not someone else's highlight reel. The agencies that win long-term are boring about this. They track their numbers, make decisions based on their numbers, and ignore most of the noise from the outside.
