What Meta's Andromeda Update Actually Means for Info Product Advertisers

Andromeda Changed the Game Quietly

Meta rolled out Andromeda and most advertisers didn't even notice. No big announcement, no flashy press release. Just a fundamental shift in how ads get delivered that changes the strategy for anyone selling info products, coaching, or high-ticket services.

Before Andromeda, Meta's ad delivery system evaluated a relatively small pool of ads to decide what to show each user. Andromeda expanded that pool dramatically — up to 10x more ads being considered for each impression. That sounds technical, but the practical implications are significant.

What It Actually Means for Your Ads

More ads being considered for each impression means more competition for every eyeball. But it also means Meta's algorithm is getting much better at finding the right person for the right ad. The system is smarter about matching, which means broad targeting works better than it used to.

For info product advertisers, this is a big deal. The old playbook of stacking detailed interest targeting and creating dozens of audience variations is becoming less effective. Meta's algorithm can now find your buyers in a broad audience better than you can with manual targeting. Fighting the algorithm with overly specific audiences is like hiring a GPS navigator and then refusing to let them use the steering wheel.

The shift is clear: creative quality and messaging matter more than ever. Targeting matters less. Your ad needs to self-select the right audience through its copy and creative, because the platform is doing less of that work through targeting settings.

How to Adapt Your Campaign Structure

The practical changes aren't complicated, but they require letting go of some habits:

  • Move toward broader audiences — Advantage+ and broad targeting are performing better post-Andromeda
  • Invest more in creative testing — when the algorithm decides based on creative fit, you need more variations
  • Simplify your campaign structure — fewer campaigns with more creative flexibility beats dozens of micro-targeted ad sets
  • Let campaigns run longer before making changes — the algorithm needs more data to optimize in the new system

The Creative Strategy Shift

When targeting did most of the work, you could get away with decent creative. Now that the algorithm shows your ad to a wider pool and relies on engagement signals to refine delivery, your creative is doing double duty. It needs to attract the right people and repel the wrong ones simultaneously.

For info product advertisers, that means your hooks need to be more specific to your ideal buyer. Not "want to grow your business?" but "if you're an info business doing $30K/month organic and you've never run a paid ad, here's what you need to know." The specificity doesn't narrow your targeting. It ensures the algorithm's broader targeting still delivers qualified eyeballs. Your copy becomes your targeting. That's the post-Andromeda reality.
