Where to Put Your Focus When Scaling

The Focus Problem at Scale

At $10K/month, you can do everything yourself and it kind of works. Sloppy but functional. At $50K/month and beyond, trying to do everything is the thing that stops you from growing. The business needs your attention on fewer, higher-impact activities. But figuring out which activities those are is where most people get stuck.

The natural instinct is to work on whatever feels urgent. Client fires, email inbox, the new idea that hit you in the shower. That approach produces a lot of motion and very little progress. You end up busy but not productive, working 12-hour days and wondering why revenue hasn't moved.

The Revenue Chain

Every business has a revenue chain — the sequence of events that turns a stranger into a paying customer. Your job when scaling is to identify the weakest link in that chain and focus there until it's no longer the weakest link. Then move to the next one.

For most info product and service businesses, the chain looks like this: traffic, conversion, sales, delivery, retention. At any given time, one of those is the bottleneck. If you're getting plenty of traffic but nobody's booking calls, conversion is the bottleneck. If everyone books calls but nobody buys, sales is the bottleneck. If everyone buys but churns in 60 days, retention is the bottleneck.

The mistake is working on non-bottleneck activities. Improving your traffic when your funnel doesn't convert is pouring water into a bucket with a hole in it. Fix the hole first.

How to Identify Your Bottleneck

It's usually simpler than you think. Look at the numbers at each stage of your revenue chain and find where the biggest drop-off is:

  • If your ad CTR is good but your landing page conversion rate is below 15%, focus on the page
  • If leads are booking but showing up at less than 70%, focus on your confirmation sequence
  • If your close rate is below 20%, focus on your sales process or lead qualification
  • If churn is above 10% monthly, focus on delivery and results

The 90-Day Rule

Once you identify your bottleneck, commit to fixing it for at least 90 days before moving on. Not because it takes that long to make changes, but because it takes that long to see whether those changes actually worked. Business owners who switch focus every two weeks never fix anything. They just rearrange deck chairs. Pick the one thing that matters most right now. Work on it daily. Measure it weekly. Evaluate it at 90 days. Then pick the next thing. This feels slow compared to juggling five priorities. But the businesses that hit $100K/month and beyond are always the ones that got one thing right at a time, in sequence, rather than getting five things half-right simultaneously.
