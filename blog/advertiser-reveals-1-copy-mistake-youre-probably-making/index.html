Advertiser Reveals #1 "copy Mistake" You're Probably Making

The Mistake Is Simpler Than You Think

After reviewing thousands of ads across hundreds of accounts, one mistake shows up more than any other. It's not bad headlines or weak CTAs. It's writing about yourself instead of writing about your prospect.

It sounds obvious. Everyone "knows" you should focus on the customer. But pull up your last three ads and count how many sentences start with "I" or "we" versus "you." Most people are shocked at how self-focused their copy is when they actually audit it.

Why It Happens

It happens because when you sit down to write an ad, your brain defaults to what you know best — your own offer. Your methodology. Your results. Your story. Your credentials. All of that feels relevant because it is relevant. But it's relevant as supporting evidence, not as the lead.

When someone scrolls past your ad, they're not thinking about you. They're thinking about themselves. Their problems, their goals, their frustrations, their ambitions. If the first thing they see is you talking about you, it doesn't register. It's noise. But if the first thing they see is a precise description of their own situation, they stop. They feel seen. And that feeling is what earns you the next sentence.

The Fix

Rewrite your ads with a simple rule: the first 2-3 sentences must be entirely about the reader's world. No mention of you, your company, your product, or your results. Just their reality.

Instead of "We've helped 200+ business owners scale past $100K/month," try "You've got the leads coming in, the product is good, and your sales calls are converting. But revenue has been stuck at the same number for three months and you can't figure out why."

See the difference? The second version describes a feeling that a specific person has right now. It creates the "that's me" moment. Once you have that moment, the reader is invested. Now they'll read your solution, your proof, and your CTA — because you earned their attention by demonstrating that you understand their world.

  • Lead with the reader's current situation, not your offer
  • Use "you" language for the first 2-3 sentences minimum
  • Introduce your solution only after you've described their problem
  • Your credentials are proof, not your opening

The Compound Effect of Getting This Right

Fixing this one mistake ripples through your entire funnel. Ads that lead with the reader's reality get higher CTRs because they stop the scroll. Higher CTRs mean lower CPMs because Meta rewards engagement. Lower CPMs mean cheaper leads. And the leads themselves are more qualified because the ad pre-selected people who identified with the specific situation you described. One copywriting shift creates a cascade of better performance at every stage. It's not the only thing that matters, but if you're only going to fix one thing about your ads today, make it this.
