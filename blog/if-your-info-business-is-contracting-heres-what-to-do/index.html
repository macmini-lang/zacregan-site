If Your Info Business is Contracting Here's What to Do

Contraction Isn't a Death Sentence

Revenue going down is scary. It triggers survival mode, and survival mode produces panic decisions — slashing prices, launching new products, firing people, or doing nothing at all while hoping it reverses on its own. None of those responses are reliably correct.

What you need first is a diagnosis. Revenue contraction has specific causes, and the fix depends entirely on which one you're dealing with. Treating a churn problem with more ad spend is like treating a leak in your boat by rowing faster. You need to find the leak.

The Three Sources of Contraction

Revenue in an info business comes from three places: new customer acquisition, existing customer retention, and average revenue per customer. When revenue contracts, one or more of those is declining. Figure out which one.

If acquisition is declining, fewer new people are buying. Your ads might be fatigued, your organic reach might have dropped, or a competitor might have entered the market with a better offer. The fix is upstream — new creative, new angles, or repositioning your offer.

If retention is declining, people are leaving faster. This is almost always a delivery or expectations problem. Either the product isn't delivering results, or the marketing promised something the product doesn't match. The fix is downstream — improve the product, reset expectations, or add touchpoints that keep people engaged.

If revenue per customer is declining, you're either discounting more or failing to ascend people into higher-ticket offers. The fix is in your pricing and upsell structure.

The Diagnostic Process

  • Pull your new customer count for the last 6 months — is it flat, growing, or declining?
  • Pull your churn rate for the same period — is it stable or increasing?
  • Calculate your average revenue per customer — has it changed?
  • Look at your ad metrics — has cost per acquisition increased?

These four data points tell you exactly where the problem is. Don't guess. Don't assume. Let the numbers point you to the right lever.

The Recovery Playbook

Once you've diagnosed the source, the fix becomes clearer. If it's acquisition, you need fresh creative and possibly fresh positioning. Not a new product — a new way of presenting the same product. If it's retention, you need to talk to the people who left. What did they expect? What did they get? Where was the gap? If it's revenue per customer, you need to evaluate your pricing and your ascension path.

The one thing you should not do is launch something new. When an info business is contracting, the instinct to create a new product feels productive but it's almost always a distraction. The underlying issue — acquisition, retention, or pricing — will affect the new product the same way it's affecting the current one. Fix the system first. Launch new products when the system is healthy.
