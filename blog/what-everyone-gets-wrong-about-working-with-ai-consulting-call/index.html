What Everyone Gets Wrong About Working With Ai (consulting Call)

AI Isn't a Magic Button

The biggest misconception about AI in business is that it replaces thinking. You plug in a prompt, out comes the answer, and you go execute it. That's not how it works, and the people who approach it that way end up with generic output that sounds like it was written by a committee of nobody.

AI is an amplifier. It amplifies whatever you feed it. If you feed it clear thinking, specific context, and good direction, the output is excellent. If you feed it vague prompts and hope for the best, you get exactly what you'd expect — vague, hopeful output that helps nobody.

The Input Quality Problem

Most people who say "AI doesn't work for my business" are really saying "I don't know how to give AI what it needs to produce good work." And that's fair. It's a new skill. But it's a skill worth developing because the gap between someone who's good at working with AI and someone who's bad at it is enormous.

Good input means providing context. Not just "write me an ad" but "write me an ad for a $15K coaching program targeting SaaS founders doing $50K-$200K/month who have never run paid ads before. Here's what makes this program different. Here's the main objection. Here's the voice it should sound like."

That level of specificity takes 60 seconds more and produces output that's 10x better. But most people skip it because they want the magic button.

Where AI Actually Shines

The highest-value use cases for AI in business aren't the flashy ones. They're the boring ones:

  • Drafting variations — you have one ad that works, AI helps you create 20 variations to test
  • Research synthesis — you have 15 sources of information, AI helps you find patterns across them
  • Process documentation — you know how something works, AI helps you write the SOP
  • Communication — you know what you want to say, AI helps you say it faster and cleaner

Notice the pattern. In every case, you're providing the knowledge and direction. AI is providing speed and consistency. It's not replacing your expertise. It's making your expertise more productive.

The Mistake That Costs the Most

The most expensive mistake isn't using AI badly. It's trusting AI output without verification. AI will confidently produce things that are wrong — statistics, claims, even code that looks right but breaks in production. If you take that output, put it in front of a client or prospect, and it turns out to be wrong, the damage to your credibility is real. Always verify anything factual. Always read the output as a critical editor, not a grateful recipient. AI is your first draft machine, not your final answer machine. The people who get the most value from AI treat it like a talented junior employee — capable of excellent work, but needs direction and review.
